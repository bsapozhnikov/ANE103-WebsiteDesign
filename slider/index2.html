Pomegranates 101: Mesopotamian Recipes in the Modern Era

The civilizations of ancient Mesopotamia developed a significant medical corpus, and their documentation of medical recipes allows modern experimenters to quantify their efficacy. A common ingredient in Mesopotamian medicines was the pomegranate; recipes called for various parts of the plant, including juice, root, and peel (Scurlock, 2014). These recipes were for the purpose of treating a range of diseases, from aches and burns to those likely caused by microbes. Modern experimenters have found that the use of pomegranates probably did assist in alleviating symptoms and curing diseases. Pomegranates have shown promise in healing second-degree burns (Ma, 2015) and in killing the periodontopathogens which cause dental plaques (Bhadbhade, Acharya, Rodrigues, & Thakur, 2011). Components of pomegranate juice which have successfully been used to kill E. Coli and other common bacteria also have been identified as candidate antimicrobial drugs (Naziri, Rajaian, & Firouzi, 2012).

Other substances mentioned often in Mesopotamian medical textbooks have also been shown to have antimicrobial effects. The leaves of mint, thyme, and other herbs were shown to significantly reduce bacterial growth (Oral, Vatansever, Guven, & Gulmez, 2008), as was cumin seed (Shetty, Singhal, & Kulkarni, 1994). Several essential oils, including cypress (Romeo, De Luca, Piscopo, & Poiana, 2008) and juniper oils, were shown to possess both antibacterial and antifungal properties (Filipowicz, Kaminski, Kurlenda, Asztemborska & Ochocka, 2003). Myrrh oil was found to act as both an anesthetic and an antibacterial agent (Dolara et al., 2000), and the omega-3 fatty acids present in fish oil are known also to have antibacterial effects (Som & Radhakrishnan, 2011). While each of these ingredients has been separately shown to be effective in killing microorganisms, it has not yet been studied whether these ingredients are more effective in combination. The purpose of our study is to test whether the treatments outlined in Mesopotamian medical textbooks are more effective than pomegranate juice alone.
